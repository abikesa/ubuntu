Intelligence and Evolution

In evolutionary biology, intelligence is often understood not as the pinnacle of development but as an adaptive mechanism that enhances survival[1]. While popular narratives sometimes portray intelligence as a crowning achievement of human evolution, some scholars argue that it functions more subtly, embedded within ecological and behavioral strategies that promote resilience across species[2].

Charles Darwin’s principle of natural selection underscores this perspective, emphasizing traits that aid survival and reproduction rather than idealized end-states[3]. Arthur C. Clarke notably remarked that "it has yet to be proved that intelligence has real survival value,"[4] capturing a broader skepticism about the evolutionary utility of intelligence beyond immediate adaptive contexts.

Contemporary research into "extended intelligence" suggests that intelligence may be better conceptualized as a distributed property of organisms and their environments, rather than a fixed trait[5]. This systemic view aligns with ecological models of cognition and evolution, where adaptability and feedback loops play central roles.
